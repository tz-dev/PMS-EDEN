1) Minimal Vignette

1.1 Title

Mediation collapse as cost-rational, not character.

1.2 Scene boundaries

  • Domain: post-breach coordination, repair exhaustion, mediation, exit, and continuity burden
  • Scope: roles, asymmetries, temporal accumulation, mediation viability, and binding dynamics

1.3 Short scene

A scene has already passed through breach and failed repair attempts. Costs have accumulated across time, and earlier mediation efforts have already consumed available coordination bandwidth. One role-position stays closer to endpoint authority and exit capacity, while another remains closer to continuity work, mediation labor, and relational carry-over. Pressure now rises to “keep talking” or “keep it together” without reopening the actual role interfaces through which burdens, obligations, and exposure would have to be redistributed. Mediation continues as a repeated script, while hard exit, distancing, or formal withdrawal increasingly appear as competing stabilization responses.

1.4 Structural focus

The case examines when mediation stops functioning as Σ-capable coordination and becomes a cost-displacement mechanism under real Ω across irreversible Θ. The decisive issue is not whether mediation is morally preferable, but whether continued mediation still changes the consequence field or merely externalizes further load.

  • Clarifies how Ψ can polarize into exit-binding on one side and carry-binding on the other.
  • Provides a clean case for distinguishing structural rationality from moral ranking in PMS-EDEN examples.

2.3 Central repo question

When does mediation remain structurally viable coordination, and when does it become a coercive endurance script that externalizes cost without reopening Σ-capable interfaces?

2.4 Lens relevance

  • Post-breach viability: The case directly instantiates PMS-EDEN's post-breach logic: once Θ and Ω make prior costs non-resettable, continued repair scripts may become structurally more expensive than termination.
  • Reciprocity conditions: The example highlights that reciprocity requires active Σ under real Ω, not indefinite continuation of mediation language.
  • Validity gate: The case is particularly useful for showing how mediation pressure can become formally invalid as PMS application when Χ is suppressed and Ψ is externalized.

The case centers on post-breach asymmetry, temporal irreversibility, mediation scripts as attractors, and the failure of integration to consolidate under continued repair pressure.

  • Δ (Difference):

    • A further distinction emerges between viable coordination and repeated mediation without interface change.
  • ∇ (Impulse):

  • □ (Frame):

    • The drifted frame increasingly asks who tried, who failed, and who owes further effort.
  • Λ (Non-Event):

    • Missing burden redistribution remains active.
    • Missing durable repair remains the decisive non-event in the scene.
  • Α (Attractor):
    • Repeated mediation loops stabilize as recurrent scripts.

  • Ω (Asymmetry):

    • Real asymmetry appears in who must carry continuity costs.

  • Θ (Temporality):
    • Prior mediation costs are no longer resettable.

  • Φ (Recontextualization):
    • Mediation may be recontextualized from a coordination attempt into a moral requirement.
    • Exit may be reframed as aggression or failure.
  • Χ (Distance):
    • Distance is the key validity test: can mediation be paused, limited, or terminated without being recoded as abandonment, hostility, or guilt?
  • Σ (Integration):
    • Integration would require reopened interfaces around burden, consequence, and obligation.
    • In the drifted scene, mediation repeats without producing durable consolidation.
  • Ψ (Self-Binding):
    • Self-binding polarizes: one side binds to exit, containment, or formal boundary.
    • The other side binds to continuity, carrying, or renewed repair.
    • Drift worsens when either binding is imposed outward as obligation.

4.3 Derived-axes projection

  • A (Awareness): Awareness rises as the cost geometry becomes clearer, especially around who carries recurring mediation load and who can terminate sooner.
  • C (Coherence): Coherence weakens because mediation continues as script while Λ remains open and the scene no longer produces actual repair consolidation.
  • R (Responsibility): Responsibility becomes unstable when mediation is confused with moral duty and recurring costs are externalized onto the continuity-proximal role-position.
  • E (Action / Enactment): Action quality drops where realizations continue but fail the Σ-carriage criterion; repeated mediation is not equivalent to integrated repair.
  • D (Dignity-in-Practice): Dignity-in-Practice is stressed when continued mediation is demanded despite exhausted repair conditions, especially where Χ is attacked and endurance is coerced.

4.4 Structural mechanism summary

  • Breach renders Ω and consequence asymmetry explicit.
  • Θ makes prior repair costs non-resettable.
  • Expected redistribution and acknowledgment do not consolidate (Λ remains open).
  • □ drifts from repair praxis toward security and fairness accounting.
  • Α stabilizes mediation loops and counter-scripts (continue vs exit).
  • Σ remains suppressed or non-consolidating.
  • Ψ polarizes into carry-binding and exit-binding.
  • Further mediation risks becoming cost displacement rather than coordination.

Concise formula:
breach -> Ω legible + Θ irreversible -> open Λ -> □security/comparison -> Α mediation scripts -> Σ low/suppressed -> Ψ polarization -> mediation compulsion or termination response

8) Validity Gate Reminder

8.1 PMS entry condition

  • Χ (Distance): Distance must remain available as reversible restraint. Pausing, formalizing, limiting, or terminating mediation must remain structurally possible without automatic moralization.
  • Reversibility: The mapping must remain scene-bound and revisable. It must not harden into global claims about who is “the avoidant one” or “the caring one.”
  • D (Dignity-in-Practice): Real Ω may be named structurally, but mediation must not become a vehicle for coercive endurance demands, humiliation, or forced carrying.

8.2 Case-specific validity test

Valid if:

  • Mediation is treated as viable only where Σ-capable interfaces can still be reopened.
  • Prior irrecoverable costs under Θ are acknowledged explicitly rather than erased through renewed effort demands.
  • Χ remains speakable and self-applied rather than punished.
  • Ψ remains self-binding and dignity-preserving rather than imposed as compulsory continuation.

Formally invalid if:

  • Mediation is moralized into an unconditional duty regardless of open Λ, blocked Σ, and accumulated Θ-cost.
  • Exit, pause, or formalization are treated as proof of deficiency rather than possible cost-containment responses.
  • Ω is denied while burden remains asymmetrically distributed.
  • PMS language is used to force continued endurance or relational labor.

What looks like commitment to repair may then function structurally as cost displacement onto the continuity-proximal role-position. 10) Extended Structural Closure

10.1

The scene begins after breach and failed repair attempts. Earlier mediation efforts have already consumed coordination bandwidth, and the consequence field has changed irreversibly across time. One role-position remains closer to endpoint authority and exit capacity, while another remains closer to continuity work, mediation labor, and relational carry-over. This does not yet imply any moral asymmetry, but it already establishes different forms of exposure and capacity under real Ω across Θ.

10.2

The decisive structural question is whether mediation still functions as coordination or has already become a repeated script that no longer changes the consequence field. In PMS-EDEN terms, mediation remains viable only so long as it can reopen Σ-capable interfaces around burden, obligation, repair viability, and reversibility. Once those interfaces no longer reopen, repeated mediation ceases to be integration and becomes a form of cost management.

10.3

The non-event Λ is central here. What remains missing is not merely a pleasant outcome or emotional reconciliation, but explicit acknowledgment of prior irreversible costs, actual burden redistribution, and durable repair consolidation. Because these remain absent, the continuation of mediation cannot be treated as proof that repair remains structurally possible.

10.4

The frame □ correspondingly drifts. What began as repair praxis tends to shift toward containment, security, and effort accounting: who kept trying, who failed, who still owes continuation, and who is now threatening rupture. In this drifted frame, mediation can become less a coordination practice than a visible performance of willingness. The scene no longer asks whether repair is still structurally viable; it asks whether continuation is still being displayed.

10.5

Attractor formation Α enters when these repeated mediation loops, renewed appeals, hard boundary moves, and effort-accounting patterns begin to stabilize locally. They reduce immediate escalation pressure, but they do not necessarily reopen actual interfaces. This is why mediation compulsion can become structurally cheaper than explicit repair work even while it becomes more expensive than its remaining repair value.

10.6

Temporality Θ intensifies the problem. Prior mediation costs are not resettable. Exhaustion, repeated effort, and accumulated fallout remain part of the scene. The longer mediation repeats without redistribution or consolidation, the more likely it becomes that continued mediation no longer coordinates costs but redistributes them toward the continuity-proximal role-position.

10.7

Real asymmetry Ω appears precisely in this uneven distribution. Exit-near role-positions often hold more capacity to terminate, formalize, or close the scene, while continuity-near role-positions absorb more repeated exposure to unresolved consequences, mediation demand, and fallout handling. The issue is not who is morally better. The issue is that the capacity to exit and the obligation to carry are distributed unequally.

10.8

Recontextualization Φ can worsen the drift when mediation is no longer read as one possible coordination attempt, but as a moral test of commitment, fairness, or willingness. Under that reframe, exit may be cast as aggression, limitation as abandonment, and formalization as refusal to care. At that point, Φ no longer supports adaptation; it protects compulsory continuation.

10.9

Distance Χ is therefore the key validity test. A PMS-valid scene must still allow mediation to be paused, limited, formalized, or terminated without automatic dignity collapse. When pause becomes moral failure, boundary becomes aggression, and formalization becomes proof of deficiency, Χ is no longer functioning as reversible restraint. The continuation demand has become coercive.

10.10

Self-binding Ψ polarizes under these conditions. One role-position may bind itself to exit, containment, or formal boundary; the other may bind itself to continuity, carrying, or renewed repair. This polarization is not itself a failure. Drift worsens when either binding is imposed outward as obligation—when one role-position is compelled to continue carrying mediation labor while the other remains relatively free to exit, close, or redefine the scene.

10.11

Integration Σ correspondingly remains low or non-consolidating. Mediation may continue visibly, but visible continuation is not equivalent to integrated repair. Where prior costs are not acknowledged, burden is not redistributed, repair viability is not reassessed, and interfaces are not reopened, repeated mediation fails the Σ-carriage criterion. It becomes coordination language without integration.

10.12

The structural conclusion follows directly: mediation is not automatically a sign of responsibility, and termination is not automatically a sign of deficiency. Once repair attempts have already saturated the available window, continued mediation under real Ω and irreversible Θ can become a carry-demand attractor rather than a path to repair. Under PMS-EDEN, the decisive question is whether mediation still changes the consequence field in a reversible and dignity-preserving way. If it does not, continued mediation risks becoming cost displacement rather than coordination.
