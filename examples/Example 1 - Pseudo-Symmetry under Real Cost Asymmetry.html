PMS–EDEN

Example 1: Pseudo-Symmetry under Real Cost Asymmetry

Case label: Equality Rhetoric vs. Irreversible Cost Gradients
Scope discipline: Structural mapping only. No person diagnosis, no moral ranking, no prescriptive claims. PMS application validity remains conditional on Χ + reversibility + D.

A. Scene Packet (Minimal Unit)

Scene ID:

  • Θ (temporality): Extended interaction over time; repeated coordination attempts with accumulating consequences.
  • □ (frame anchor): Initially □praxis (task/coordination-oriented), later drifting toward □comparison (equivalence/justification-oriented).
  • Roles (not persons):
    • Role R₁: proximity to end-point decisions and closure authority.
    • Role R₂: proximity to continuity work, relational maintenance, and fallout handling.

Trigger (Δ/∇):

  • Δ: Unequal exposure to irreversible consequences becomes salient.
  • ∇: Pressure to “restore fairness” without reopening coordination interfaces.

Λ candidates (expected but missing):

  • Explicit coordination of asymmetrical cost distribution.
  • Explicit renegotiation of roles and obligations under changed consequences.

Observed realizations (neutral):

  • Increased rhetoric of equality and sameness.
  • Repeated comparison and justification cycles.
  • No durable interface change.

B. Drift Trace (Operator-Readable)

Drift = □praxis → Ω-legible → □comparison → PS (pseudo-symmetry) → Α (stabilized comparison scripts) → Σ low/simulated → Ψ misbound

Key transitions:

  • Ω (asymmetry) becomes legible in consequences but is treated as illegitimate to name.
  • □ (frame) shifts from coordination to comparison.
  • Pseudo-symmetry (PS) substitutes for integration: rhetorical equality replaces functional coordination.
  • Α (attractor) stabilizes repeated comparison, justification, and leveling attempts.
  • Σ (integration) remains low or simulated; interfaces do not consolidate.
  • Ψ (self-binding) attaches to appearance of fairness rather than to coordinated obligations.

C. Cost Distribution (Structural, Not Moral)

Irreversible vs. relational costs:

  • R₁-adjacent costs:
    • Point-of-decision costs; closure achieved with limited ongoing exposure.
  • R₂-adjacent costs:
    • Ongoing relational, repair, and continuity costs across Θ.

Cost asymmetry pattern:

  • End-point roles absorb decision costs once.
  • Continuity roles absorb maintenance costs repeatedly.

System effect:

  • Pseudo-symmetry obscures this distribution, shifting real costs into invisible, durable load.

D. Rational Response Envelope (Structural Reasonableness)

Under this cost profile, the following responses are structurally rational (not morally preferred):

  • Comparison escalation where coordination is blocked and Ω is taboo.
  • Persistence in maintenance work where exit would increase relational fallout.
  • Withdrawal or mediation refusal where continuation becomes irreversibly expensive.

These responses follow cost logic under Ω across Θ, not character traits or virtues.

E. Dignity-in-Practice Stress Test (D under Ω)

Risk point:

  • “Equality” rhetoric becomes a shield that legitimizes coercion-by-frame when it demands sameness while ignoring real gradients.

Failure mode:

  • Ψ→Other: Binding is externalized as “you must be fair / equal,” bypassing Χ.
  • Χ (distance) is attacked as “avoidance” or “lack of commitment.”

Structural indicator:

  • Appeals to fairness function as emergency justification to suppress distance and force compliance.

F. Reader-Guard (Misinterpretation Prevention)

This example does not claim:

  • that equality is undesirable,
  • that any role is morally superior,
  • that cost-bearing implies virtue or victimhood,
  • that asymmetry implies blame.

It does claim:

  • that equality rhetoric can function as a frame substitution,
  • that pseudo-symmetry hides real cost gradients,
  • that coercion can arise without moral language, purely through frame control.

G. PMS Validity Check (Application Gate)

Any attempt to use this analysis for action or binding is PMS-valid only if:

  • Χ (distance) is preserved as reversible restraint,
  • real Ω (asymmetry) is nameable and coordinable,
  • D (dignity-in-practice) constrains how asymmetry is handled,
  • binding remains Ψ→Self, not enforced as Ψ→Other.

Absent these conditions, the analysis may still describe the situation, but any prescriptive use is formally invalid as PMS application.

H. Structural Closure (Non-Normative)

From this configuration, a clear structural consequence follows: once equality rhetoric replaces coordination under real Ω (asymmetry) across Θ (temporality), the system no longer negotiates costs—it redistributes them invisibly.

Pseudo-symmetry does not resolve asymmetry; it stabilizes it by relocating consequences from explicit interfaces into durable relational load. What appears as fairness maintenance functions structurally as a cost-masking attractor (Α).

At this stage, further comparison does not reopen coordination. Instead, it increases justification pressure, accelerates leveling cycles, and raises the likelihood of coercion-by-frame. Χ (distance) becomes structurally threatened—not due to deficient intent, but because the dominant □ (frame) no longer tolerates restraint.

This outcome is neither a failure of character nor of intention. It is the predictable result of maintaining equivalence talk where asymmetrical consequences are already irreversible.
